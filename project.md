1. 🎯 Problem StatementThe "Trust" Dilemma: Public health policy in India currently oscillates between data-driven forecasting (ML) and epidemiological theory (Mechanistic). However, TB is a unique challenge: it has a long latency period and significant under-reporting.Our Core Question: When the ML model (Prophet) predicts a decline based on reported notification trends, but the mechanistic model ($SEI_LR$) predicts a resurgence due to "hidden" transmission in the latent reservoir, which should a policymaker trust? We hypothesize that the answer depends entirely on the spatial context (state-level infrastructure).2. 🌍 Study Regions & Spatial HeterogeneityTo satisfy the requirement for "contrasting regions," we have selected:Kerala: High-quality surveillance, high literacy, and a robust public health system. (Expected: High model alignment).Bihar: High population density, resource constraints, and a large "missing" middle (private sector cases not in official data). (Expected: High model disagreement).Uttar Pradesh: Massive geographic scale and diverse socio-economic pockets. (Expected: Test of spatial variance).3. 🗺️ Mandatory Geospatial Analysis (The "Pre-Modeling" Phase)Reasoning: We cannot model what we haven't mapped. Spatial analysis identifies the "data-reliability" of each region.Planned Visualizations:Choropleth A (Notification Rate): TB cases per 100,000. This normalizes the data, preventing the "population bias" where UP looks worse than Kerala simply because it is larger.Choropleth B (Infrastructure Density): Number of Diagnostic Centers per 10,000 people.Spatial Analysis Goal: Identify if TB "Hotspots" correlate with high density or low healthcare access. This informs the $\beta$ (transmission) and $\gamma$ (recovery) parameters in our mechanistic model.4. ⚙️ Model 1: The Mechanistic Model ($SEI_LR$)Reasoning: Standard SIR/SEIR models fail for TB because they ignore the dormant stage.The Compartments:S (Susceptible): Healthy population.E (Exposed/Early Progression): Recently infected; high risk of becoming sick.$I$ (Infectious): Active cases spreading TB bacteria.$L$ (Latent/Slow Progression): The "Hidden Reservoir". These individuals carry TB but aren't sick yet.R (Recovered): Those who completed the 6-month DOTS treatment.Key Mathematical Feature:We include a Reactivation Term ($\omega$). This captures people moving from $L \to I$ due to age or malnutrition. This is the factor ML cannot see.5. 🤖 Model 2: The Machine Learning Model (Prophet)Reasoning: We need a model that understands the "human" patterns of data.Why Prophet?Seasonality: TB in India peaks in Spring (Q1/Q2) and dips in Winter (Q4). Prophet handles this automatically.The "COVID Hole": We will use Prophet's "change-point" detection to treat the massive notification drop in 2020 as a reporting anomaly rather than a biological reality.Features: Time-series of historical cases, population density, and state-wise poverty indices.6. ⚠️ Identified Problems & Refined SolutionsReasoning: Every project faces data hurdles. Proposing solutions shows high-level reasoning.Problem FacedRefinement / StrategyNotification Gaps: Bihar's data likely misses 30% of cases.We will adjust the $SEI_LR$ model to include an "Under-detection Factor" derived from WHO national estimates.Private Sector Noise: Many patients use private clinics and aren't in the Ni-kshay data.We will use the "TB Outcome" dataset to estimate the private-to-public ratio for parameter calibration.Parameter Uncertainty: Values like "Transmission Rate" change per state.We will perform Sensitivity Analysis: if we change transmission by 10%, how much does our policy advice change?7. 📈 Disagreement & Policy Interpretation (The "Core")The "Disagreement" Deliverable:We will produce a dashboard showing where the $SEI_LR$ curve deviates from the ML forecast.Policy Recommendation Framework:Trust ML (Prophet) in Kerala: When data is high-quality, ML is excellent for short-term logistics (e.g., predicting drug stockouts).Trust Mechanistic ($SEI_LR$) in Bihar/UP: When data is patchy, we must trust biological principles. If the model shows a rise in the "Latent" ($L$) pool, the government must increase spending even if reported cases are flat.The Risk: Relying only on ML in Bihar leads to a "false sense of security," resulting in budget cuts right before a reactivation surge.8. 📊 Data Resources SummaryIncidence Data: Ni-kshay Dashboard (Official Gov.
